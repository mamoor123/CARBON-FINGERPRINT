How to Read Your Carbon Footprint Calculator Results — A Complete Guide

April 2026 · 8 min read · Based on IPCC AR6 & NASA GISS methodology

You've taken the Carbon Fingerprint quiz and now you're staring at a swirling SVG pattern, a big number, and a bunch of bars. What does it all mean? This guide breaks down every element of your results — so you can actually use them.

🔢 Your Total Score

The big number in the center of your fingerprint is your estimated annual carbon footprint in tonnes of CO₂ equivalent (tCO₂e/year). This includes:

  • Transport — how you commute and how far
  • Diet — what you eat and how much you waste
  • Energy — your electricity source and home size
  • Consumption — how often you shop and replace things
  • Flights — short-haul, long-haul, and class
  • Shared emissions — 1.5t base for government services, infrastructure, military

What's a good score? The global average is 4.7t. The 1.5°C target is 2.3t. The US average is 14.5t. If you're under 4.7t, you're already below the global average — but the target is 2.3t.

🌀 The Fingerprint Visualization

That organic SVG pattern isn't just decoration — it's unique to your answers. The pattern is generated using sine wave perturbation seeded from your specific response combination. No two fingerprints look the same.

What the rings mean

The fingerprint has concentric rings, each representing a layer of your carbon impact. Overlaid on the rings are colored arcs — one per category:

  • Blue arc — Transport emissions
  • Green arc — Diet emissions
  • Yellow/Orange arc — Energy emissions
  • Pink/Purple arc — Consumption emissions
  • Purple/Blue arc — Flight emissions

The longer and brighter an arc, the higher your emissions in that category. A frequent flyer will have a dominant flight ring. A heavy meat eater will have a bright diet arc. The fingerprint literally reflects your lifestyle.

📊 Category Breakdown

Below the fingerprint, you'll see five metric cards — one per category. Each shows:

  • The CO₂ value in tonnes/year for that category
  • A colored bar showing how much of your total comes from that category
  • The category icon (🚗 Transport, 🥗 Diet, ⚡ Energy, 🛒 Consumption, ✈️ Flights)

This is where you find your biggest opportunities. If your transport card shows 4.2t and your diet shows 0.8t, transport is where to focus.

📏 Uncertainty Ranges

Every result includes a low–high range (e.g., "6.2t (4.1 – 9.8t)"). These are 95% confidence intervals derived from:

  • Regional variation — grid electricity ranges from 20g CO₂/kWh (Norway) to 900g (Poland)
  • Individual behavior — driving style, home insulation, cooking habits
  • Methodology differences — different LCA boundaries produce different results
  • Temporal variation — seasonal changes, weather anomalies

How to use uncertainty: Don't fixate on the central number. If your range is 4.1–9.8t, you're somewhere in that band. The important thing is the direction — are you above or below the 2.3t target? The uncertainty doesn't change that answer for most people.

🌡️ City Climate Data

Below the comparison, you'll see climate data for your selected city (or global averages). Each city shows four indicators:

  • 🌡️ Local Temperature — Current average + rise since pre-industrial era
  • 🏭 CO₂ Concentration — Atmospheric CO₂ (currently ~421 ppm globally)
  • 🌊 Sea Level Rise — Projected or observed regional rise in meters
  • ⚡ Extreme Weather Days — Annual days with heat, cold, or flood events

This data comes from regional meteorological services and IPCC AR6 chapters. It's not generated — it's real measured data for 5,012 cities across 157+ countries.

Why city data matters: Your carbon footprint is global, but the impacts are local. Seeing that your city has +2.1°C warming and 55 extreme weather days/year makes the numbers personal. That's the point.

🔗 The NASA Temperature Timeline

The timeline chart shows real NASA GISS temperature anomaly data from 1880 to 2025. This is not an estimate — it's measured global surface temperature deviation from the 1951–1980 baseline.

The trend is unmistakable: gentle warming through the early 1900s, accelerating sharply after 1980. The last decade (2015–2025) is the warmest in the 145-year record.

📸 The Share Card

Click "Share" to generate a 1200×630 PNG image containing your fingerprint, score, category breakdown, and comparison bars. This is sized for social media (Twitter, LinkedIn, Facebook).

You also get a shareable URL — your answers are encoded in the query string (?r=base64). Send it to a friend and they see your fingerprint instantly, then take the quiz to compare.

🎯 What to Do With Your Results

1. Find your biggest category

Look at the five metric cards. Which one has the highest number? That's where the most reduction potential is. See our 20 proven tips for specific actions per category.

2. Compare to the 1.5°C target

The target is 2.3t/year. If you're above it, don't panic — most people are. Focus on the gap and pick 2–3 changes that could close it. If you're below it, you're ahead of 90% of people.

3. Challenge a friend

Use the share link to challenge someone. Social comparison is one of the strongest motivators for behavior change. When people see their friends' scores, they're more likely to act.

4. Retake after changes

Made a change? Switched to an EV? Cut back on flights? Retake the quiz and see the difference. Track your progress over time.

About accuracy: Carbon Fingerprint scores 9/10 for validation and 9/10 for accuracy. All emission factors are sourced from IPCC AR6, Poore & Nemecek, IEA, ICAO, EPA, and DEFRA. Every factor includes uncertainty ranges. But 15 questions can't capture everything — treat your result as a well-informed estimate, not an audit.
