144 Years of NASA Climate Data: The Story Behind the Numbers

Updated April 2026 · 11 min read · Data: NASA GISS GISTEMP v4, NOAA, Mauna Loa Observatory

Since 1880, NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies (GISS) has been tracking global surface temperatures. That's 144 years of continuous data — the longest, most comprehensive climate record we have.

This isn't modeling. It's not projection. It's what actually happened — measured by thousands of weather stations, ships, and buoys around the world.

The Temperature Timeline: 1880–2025

The story of 144 years of climate data falls into three clear chapters:

Chapter 1: Slow Change (1880–1940)

Global temperatures rose roughly 0.1°C over 60 years. Industrialization was spreading, but emissions were still small compared to today. CO₂ went from ~280 ppm to ~310 ppm.

Chapter 2: The Mid-Century Plateau (1940–1975)

Temperatures roughly flatlined — even dipped slightly. This confused scientists at the time. We now know it was caused by aerosol pollution (sulfate particles from coal) reflecting sunlight. The warming was being masked.

Chapter 3: The Acceleration (1975–2025)

This is when everything changed. The warming signal broke through the aerosol mask. Since 1975, temperatures have risen ~1.0°C — a rate 10x faster than any natural warming in the past 10,000 years.

CO₂: From 280 to 421 ppm

For 800,000 years, atmospheric CO₂ oscillated between 180 and 280 ppm — driven by ice ages and warm periods. Then humans started burning fossil fuels:

  • 1750 (pre-industrial): ~280 ppm
  • 1958 (Keeling Curve begins): 315 ppm — Charles Keeling starts continuous measurements at Mauna Loa
  • 1988: 350 ppm — NASA scientist James Hansen testifies to Congress about climate change
  • 2013: 400 ppm — Crossed for the first time in human history
  • 2024: 421 ppm — Highest in at least 800,000 years, likely 3–5 million years

The rate of increase is itself increasing. It took ~200 years to go from 280 to 350 ppm (1750–1988). It took just 36 years to go from 350 to 421 ppm (1988–2024).

What Does This Mean for You?

The 1.2°C of warming we've already locked in is already causing:

  • More extreme heat days — Heat waves that used to happen once every 50 years now happen every 10
  • Rising seas — ~20cm since 1900, accelerating to 3.7mm/year now
  • Shifting seasons — Growing seasons changing, ecosystems moving
  • More intense storms — Warmer oceans = more energy for hurricanes

Every fraction of a degree matters. The difference between 1.5°C and 2°C of warming is:

  • 70% more people exposed to severe heat
  • 2x the loss of plant and animal species
  • 10 million more people affected by sea level rise
  • $10+ trillion more in economic damages
