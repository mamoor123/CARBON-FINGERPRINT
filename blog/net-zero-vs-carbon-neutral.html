Net Zero vs Carbon Neutral: What's the Difference? (2026)

Updated April 2026 · 7 min read · Plain-English guide to climate jargon

Every company claims to be "going green." But what do carbon neutral, net zero, climate positive, and carbon negative actually mean? And — more importantly — which ones are real and which ones are greenwashing?

The Four Terms, Explained Simply

Easiest to achieve

🌍 Carbon Neutral

What it means: You're offsetting the CO₂ you emit — typically by buying carbon credits (planting trees, funding renewable projects). You haven't necessarily reduced your actual emissions.

Covers: Only CO₂ (not methane, nitrous oxide, etc.)

The catch: You can claim "carbon neutral" by simply buying offsets without changing anything about how you operate. This is why it's sometimes called the easy way out.

The gold standard

🎯 Net Zero

What it means: You've reduced emissions as much as possible across your entire value chain, and only offset what you truly can't eliminate. The emphasis is on reduction first.

Covers: All greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, etc.)

The standard: The Paris Agreement sets a global net zero target by 2050. The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) validates company net zero claims.

Going further

⚡ Climate Positive / Carbon Negative

What it means: You remove more greenhouse gases from the atmosphere than you emit. You're not just neutral — you're making things better.

Example: Microsoft has pledged to be carbon negative by 2030. They plan to remove all the CO₂ they've ever emitted since 1975 by 2050.

Watch out

🌿 Carbon Positive

Confusing, right? "Carbon positive" means the same thing as "climate positive" in some contexts — removing more than you emit. But some companies use it loosely to mean "doing good things for the climate." Always check the fine print.

How to Spot Greenwashing

When a company claims "carbon neutral" or "net zero," ask these questions:

  1. What's included? — Scope 1 (direct)? Scope 2 (electricity)? Scope 3 (supply chain)? Real net zero covers Scope 3.
  2. What's the timeline? — "Net zero by 2023" usually means heavy offsetting. "Net zero by 2040 with interim targets" means real reduction.
  3. Who verified it? — SBTi-validated claims are trustworthy. Self-declared claims are not.
  4. What offsets are used? — Avoided deforestation credits are controversial. Direct air capture and biochar are higher quality.

💡 For individuals: You can aim for carbon neutral by offsetting your unavoidable emissions. But the real goal is reduction — cut what you can first, offset the rest.
