Carbon Credits Explained: How They Work & Where to Buy (2026)

Updated April 2026 · 10 min read · No jargon, just honest answers

Carbon credits are everywhere — in corporate sustainability reports, airline booking pages, and climate policy debates. But what actually is a carbon credit? How do carbon credits work? And can you actually buy them as an individual?

This guide breaks down everything you need to know about carbon credits in 2026: the carbon credit definition, how the market works, types of credits, where to buy them, and whether they're genuinely worth your money.

What Are Carbon Credits?

A carbon credit is a tradable certificate that represents the right to emit — or the removal of — one metric tonne of carbon dioxide (CO₂) or its equivalent in other greenhouse gases. Think of it as a permit: one credit equals one tonne of CO₂.

Carbon credits exist in two main markets:

  • Compliance markets — Government-regulated systems (like the EU Emissions Trading System) where companies are legally required to hold credits for their emissions
  • Voluntary markets — Where companies, organizations, and individuals choose to buy credits to offset emissions beyond legal requirements

The concept is straightforward: if you can't reduce your emissions to zero, you can fund projects that reduce or remove CO₂ elsewhere to compensate. That compensation is measured in carbon credits.

💡 Carbon credit definition: A carbon credit is a transferable instrument certified by governments or independent bodies, representing the reduction or removal of one metric tonne of CO₂ equivalent from the atmosphere.

How Do Carbon Credits Work?

Understanding how carbon credits work means following the lifecycle from creation to retirement:

Step 1: A Project Reduces or Removes Emissions

A project developer creates a carbon reduction initiative — a wind farm, a reforestation effort, a methane capture facility at a landfill, or a direct air capture plant. These projects must demonstrate that they actually reduce or remove CO₂ from the atmosphere beyond what would have happened without the project.

Step 2: Third-Party Verification

Independent auditors verify the project against established standards like Gold Standard, Verra (VCS), or the American Carbon Registry. They confirm:

  • Additionality — The reduction wouldn't have happened without carbon credit funding
  • Permanence — The carbon stays sequestered (trees won't be immediately cut down)
  • No leakage — The project doesn't just push emissions elsewhere
  • Accurate measurement — The claimed tonnage of CO₂ reduction is real

Step 3: Credits Are Issued

Once verified, credits are registered on a public registry (like Verra's Verra Registry or Gold Standard's Impact Registry). Each credit gets a unique serial number to prevent double counting.

Step 4: Credits Are Sold

Credits are sold on exchanges (like Xpansiv CBL or AirCarbon Exchange), through brokers, or directly from project developers. Buyers include corporations meeting net-zero targets, governments fulfilling compliance obligations, and individuals offsetting personal emissions.

Step 5: Credits Are Retired

When a buyer uses a credit to offset emissions, it is retired on the registry — meaning it can never be resold or double-counted. This is the final, critical step that ensures the credit represents a real, one-time offset.

Carbon Credits vs Carbon Offsets

You'll hear "carbon credits" and "carbon offsets" used interchangeably, and in most conversations that's fine. But technically there's a distinction:

  • Carbon credits are the tradable units — the certificates themselves, representing one tonne of CO₂
  • Carbon offsets are the underlying actions — the actual projects that reduce or remove emissions

In practice, when someone says "I bought carbon credits," they mean they purchased credits generated by offset projects. The terms overlap so heavily that most people — and most of the industry — treat them as synonyms. If you want the deep dive, check our guide on carbon offsets explained.

The key takeaway: whether you call them credits or offsets, what matters is quality and verification. A cheap, unverified credit is worthless regardless of the label.

How to Buy Carbon Credits

Ready to buy carbon credits? Here's how to do it properly in 2026:

1. Calculate Your Footprint First

Before buying anything, know what you're offsetting. Calculate your carbon fingerprint to understand your annual emissions across transport, energy, diet, and consumption.

2. Choose a Reputable Platform

Not all sellers are equal. Stick with platforms that offer verified credits from recognized standards:

  • Gold Standard Marketplace — Widely considered the gold standard (literally) for credit quality
  • Verra Registry — The world's largest voluntary carbon credit program
  • South Pole — Major project developer and credit retailer
  • Atmosfair — German non-profit specializing in high-quality offsets
  • Pachama — Tech-driven platform using satellite monitoring to verify forest projects

3. Look for These Quality Markers

  • ✅ Third-party verification (Gold Standard, Verra VCS, ACR)
  • ✅ Additionality — Would the project exist without credit funding?
  • ✅ Permanence guarantee — Buffer pools or insurance against reversal
  • ✅ Transparent registry — Credits have serial numbers and can be tracked
  • ✅ Recent vintages — Credits from recent years (2022+) are more reliable

4. Decide How Much to Buy

Credits are sold per tonne. If your footprint is 4.7 tonnes (global average), you'd buy 4–5 credits. Most platforms let you purchase fractional amounts or round up.

5. Retire the Credits

Make sure your credits are retired (permanently removed from circulation) in your name. A certificate of retirement is your proof that the offset actually happened.

Are Carbon Credits Worth It?

The honest answer: it depends on how you use them.

When Carbon Credits Are Worth It

  • You've already reduced what you can (switched to renewables, changed diet, cut flights) and want to offset the remainder
  • You buy verified credits from Gold Standard or Verra — not cheap, unverified ones
  • You treat credits as a complement to reduction, not a replacement for it
  • You fund removal projects (reforestation, biochar, DAC) over avoidance projects when budget allows
  • You want to support climate projects that need funding to exist

When Carbon Credits Are NOT Worth It

  • You buy the cheapest credits ($1–5/tonne) with no verification — these often represent phantom reductions
  • You use credits as an excuse to keep polluting without changing behavior
  • You buy from platforms with no transparency about project details or verification standards
  • You don't verify that credits were actually retired in your name

A 2023 investigation by The Guardian found that over 90% of certain Verra-certified rainforest credits didn't represent real carbon reductions. This doesn't mean all credits are worthless — it means you need to be selective. Quality costs more, but cheap credits often deliver nothing.

The Golden Rule: Reduce First, Offset Second

Carbon credits are a tool, not a magic wand. The most effective approach:

  1. Measure — Calculate your carbon footprint to understand where your emissions come from
  2. Reduce — Cut what you can (transport, energy, diet, consumption) — see our 20 practical tips
  3. Offset — Buy verified carbon credits for the emissions you truly can't eliminate

💡 Our recommendation: Calculate your footprint, reduce what you can, then offset the rest with Gold Standard or Verra verified credits. Budget ~$15–30 per tonne for quality credits that actually make a difference.
