Why Are Some Countries So Much Higher?

Three main factors drive per-capita differences:

  1. Energy mix — Countries running on coal (Australia, Poland) emit far more than those with nuclear (France) or hydro (Norway, Sweden).
  2. Wealth and consumption — Higher GDP means more cars, bigger homes, more stuff. But it's not destiny — France has a similar standard of living to Germany with 40% lower per-capita emissions.
  3. Industry and exports — Manufacturing-heavy economies (China, South Korea) have high production-based emissions. Some of those goods are consumed elsewhere.

Consumption vs Production Emissions

Production-based numbers (above) count emissions where they happen. Consumption-based numbers reassign emissions to where goods are consumed. This matters because:

  • China's production emissions are ~30% higher than consumption emissions (they manufacture for the world)
  • The US and UK consumption emissions are ~10% higher than production (they import goods)
  • The global total is the same either way — it's just about who "owns" the emissions

Historical Emissions: Who Caused the Problem?

Current per-capita numbers don't tell the full story. Cumulative historical emissions matter because CO₂ stays in the atmosphere for centuries.

  • United States — 25% of all historical CO₂ despite being 4% of world population
  • EU-27 — 22% of historical emissions
  • China — 13% historically, but now the largest annual emitter
  • India — 3% of historical emissions despite being 18% of world population
