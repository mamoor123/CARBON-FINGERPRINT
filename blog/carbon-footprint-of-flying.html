Carbon Footprint of Flying: How Bad Is a Flight Really?

Updated April 2026 · 9 min read · Data: ICAO Carbon Calculator, IPCC AR6, Our World in Data

Aviation produces about 2.5% of global CO₂ emissions — a small percentage, but concentrated among a small group of people. Only ~11% of the world's population flew in a given year, and just 1% of the world's population causes 50% of aviation emissions.

If aviation were a country, it would rank 6th in the world for CO₂ emissions — between Germany and South Korea.

Class Makes a Huge Difference

Business and first class aren't just more expensive — they're far worse for the climate:

ClassCO₂ multiplierNYC → London
Economy1x (baseline)0.9 tonnes
Premium Economy1.6x1.4 tonnes
Business3x2.6 tonnes
First4x3.5 tonnes

The reason: wider seats = fewer passengers = each person's share of the flight's emissions goes up. A business class seat on a transatlantic flight produces more CO₂ than the average person in India produces in an entire year.

Non-CO₂ Effects: The Multiplier Problem

Aviation's real climate impact is 2–4x higher than CO₂ alone. This is because planes also create:

  • Contrails — ice clouds that trap heat, especially at night
  • NOₓ emissions — create ozone (warming) at high altitude
  • Water vapor — potent greenhouse gas at cruising altitude
  • Soot particles — absorb sunlight and warm the atmosphere

The IPCC AR6 recommends multiplying aviation CO₂ by 1.9–4.2x to account for these effects. Using a 2x multiplier, that transatlantic flight is really ~1.8 tonnes, not 0.9.

How to Reduce Your Flight Emissions

  1. Fly less — The most effective option. Take trains for trips under 800km.
  2. Choose direct flights — Takeoff and landing are the most fuel-intensive phases.
  3. Fly economy — 3x lower emissions than business class.
  4. Pack light — Every kg of luggage burns more fuel.
  5. Choose newer aircraft — A350s and 787s are 20–25% more fuel efficient than older planes.
  6. Offset what you can't avoid — Use Gold Standard verified offsets (~$20–30 per tonne).

💡 Fun fact: If you skip just one transatlantic round trip per year, you save more CO₂ than switching to a vegetarian diet for an entire year.
