CO2 Emissions by Country 2026: Complete Global Ranking

Updated April 2026 · 10 min read · Data from Global Carbon Project & IEA

Which country emits the most CO2? The answer depends on how you measure it. China leads in total emissions, but countries like Qatar and Bahrain dominate per capita rankings. Meanwhile, the United States and India occupy unique positions in between. This guide breaks down carbon emissions by country using the latest 2023–2024 data from the Global Carbon Project, so you can see exactly where every nation stands.

Understanding CO2 emissions by country is crucial for climate policy, personal accountability, and knowing where the biggest opportunities for reduction lie. Let's dig into the numbers.

The top five emitters — China, the US, India, Russia, and Japan — together account for nearly 60% of all global CO2 emissions. This concentration means that climate action in these five nations alone could make an enormous difference.

It's worth noting that China's per capita emissions (6.2 tonnes) are lower than the US (14.5 tonnes), even though China's total output is more than double. This is because China has 1.4 billion people — roughly four times the US population. Which number matters more? Both. Total emissions drive the climate; per capita emissions reveal equity and responsibility.

CO2 Emissions by Region

Looking at emissions by region helps paint the bigger picture and reveals patterns that country-level data can obscure.

Asia-Pacific

The Asia-Pacific region is the world's largest emitter, responsible for over 52% of global CO2 emissions. China and India dominate this region, but countries like Indonesia, Vietnam, and Bangladesh are rapidly increasing their emissions as they industrialize. Coal remains the primary energy source across much of the region, though China is simultaneously leading the world in renewable energy deployment — installing more solar capacity in 2023 than the rest of the world combined.

The challenge here is immense: lifting billions of people out of poverty while simultaneously decarbonizing. Countries like South Korea and Japan have committed to net-zero targets but continue to rely heavily on coal and gas in the short term.

North America

The United States and Canada together emit roughly 15% of global CO2. Both countries have high per capita emissions driven by car-dependent lifestyles, large homes, energy-intensive industries, and historically cheap fossil fuels. The good news: US emissions have been declining slowly since their peak in 2005, driven by coal-to-gas switching and renewable energy growth. Canada's emissions have been flat, with oil sands production offsetting gains elsewhere.

Mexico emits around 0.47 Gt CO₂ annually — significant but far below its northern neighbors per capita, at roughly 3.6 tonnes per person.

Europe

Europe accounts for about 9% of global emissions, down significantly from its historical share. The EU has cut emissions by roughly 34% since 1990 through aggressive climate policy, renewable energy deployment, and carbon pricing via the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). Germany remains the largest European emitter but has accelerated its coal phase-out. The UK has cut emissions by over 50% since 1990 — one of the most dramatic reductions of any major economy.

Eastern European countries like Poland and Czech Republic still rely heavily on coal, creating a two-speed transition within the continent.

Middle East & North Africa

This region has the highest per capita emissions in the world but relatively low total emissions (about 7% of global output). Countries like Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, and the UAE have enormous per-person footprints driven by oil and gas production, energy-intensive desalination, and air conditioning in extreme heat. Saudi Arabia is the region's largest total emitter.

The irony: these nations are both major fossil fuel exporters and among the most climate-vulnerable, facing extreme heat that could make some areas uninhabitable by mid-century.

Sub-Saharan Africa

Sub-Saharan Africa contributes just 3–4% of global CO2 emissions despite having over 1.2 billion people. Per capita emissions are typically below 1 tonne — well under the 1.5°C target. However, emissions are growing rapidly as economies develop and populations expand. Africa is expected to account for the majority of global population growth through 2100.

The key question for Africa isn't about reducing emissions — it's about whether the continent can industrialize using clean energy rather than repeating the fossil-fuel-dependent path of other regions. Investment in solar, wind, and hydroelectric power will be critical.

Latin America

Latin America emits about 5% of global CO2, with Brazil as the dominant emitter. The region has a relatively clean electricity mix thanks to extensive hydropower, but deforestation — particularly in the Amazon — is a massive source of emissions that isn't captured in standard fossil fuel statistics. When land-use change is included, Brazil's emissions are significantly higher.

Mexico, Argentina, and Colombia are the other major emitters in the region, with transport and industry as primary drivers.

The 1.5°C Target: What It Means for Emissions

The Paris Agreement set a goal of limiting global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. To stay on track, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) says global CO2 emissions need to fall by roughly 43% by 2030 compared to 2019 levels, and reach net zero by around 2050.

What does that look like in practice?

  • Global average per capita emissions must drop from ~4.7 tonnes to ~2.3 tonnes by 2030
  • US emissions need to fall from 14.5 to roughly 5–6 tonnes per capita — a 60% cut
  • China's emissions need to peak immediately and decline rapidly — reaching roughly 3–4 tonnes per capita
  • India faces the hardest equity challenge: its per capita emissions (1.9 tonnes) are already below the 1.5°C target, but its total emissions are still rising as the economy grows
  • Europe is closest to the target trajectory but still needs to roughly halve its per capita emissions

Here's the uncomfortable truth: no major economy is currently on track for the 1.5°C target. The UN Emissions Gap Report 2024 found that current policies put the world on a path to 2.5–2.9°C of warming by 2100 — a level that would trigger catastrophic and potentially irreversible climate impacts.

The gap between pledges and action remains enormous. Many countries have announced net-zero targets but haven't enacted the policies needed to achieve them. Carbon pricing, fossil fuel subsidy reform, renewable energy mandates, and energy efficiency standards are the tools that turn pledges into reality.

Which Countries Are Making Progress?

A few bright spots exist:

  • United Kingdom — Cut emissions 50%+ since 1990 through coal phase-out and offshore wind
  • Denmark — Over 80% of electricity from renewables, targeting 100% by 2030
  • Costa Rica — Nearly 99% renewable electricity for multiple years running
  • EU overall — 34% reduction since 1990 with continued decline
  • United States — Slow but steady decline from 2005 peak, accelerated by Inflation Reduction Act

But these gains are being partially offset by rising emissions in rapidly developing economies, particularly in South and Southeast Asia.

What You Can Do

Country-level data can feel abstract and overwhelming. It's tempting to think, "I'm just one person — what difference does it make?" But understanding emissions by country is actually empowering, because it shows you where the biggest levers for change are — both in policy and in personal behavior.

Here's what actually moves the needle:

  1. Know your number. Calculate your carbon fingerprint to see how your personal emissions compare to your country's average and the 1.5°C target. You can't improve what you don't measure.
  2. Focus on the big three. For most people in developed countries, transport, home energy, and diet account for 70–80% of personal emissions. Flying less, switching to renewable electricity, and eating less meat have outsized impacts.
  3. Vote and advocate. Individual action matters, but systemic change matters more. Support politicians and policies that price carbon, accelerate renewables, and phase out fossil fuels. Your vote is your most powerful climate tool.
  4. Talk about it. Social norms shift when people discuss climate change openly. Share what you're doing, ask questions, and help normalize low-carbon choices in your community.
  5. Support developing nations. Climate justice means recognizing that countries with low per capita emissions — especially in Africa and South Asia — deserve financial and technical support to develop cleanly. Look into organizations funding renewable energy access in the Global South.

The countries at the top of these rankings have the greatest responsibility to act. But everyone — regardless of where they live — has a role to play in bending the emissions curve downward.
