Carbon Footprint of Streaming: Netflix, YouTube & Spotify

Updated April 2026 · 6 min read · Data: The Shift Project, IEA, Netflix ESG Report

Headlines love to say things like "Watching Netflix for 30 minutes produces as much CO₂ as driving 6 miles." But is that actually true?

Short answer: No. Those claims are based on outdated data that has since been debunked. Here are the real numbers.

The Debunked "6 Miles" Claim

The original viral claim came from The Shift Project in 2019. They estimated streaming produced 3.2 kg of CO₂ per hour — a number that was off by 90x.

The error: they used an outdated estimate of network energy per byte (8.7 kWh/GB) instead of the actual measured figure (~0.05 kWh/GB). The IEA corrected this in 2020.

Corrected reality: 30 minutes of Netflix HD ≈ 36g CO₂. That's about 150 meters of driving, not 6 miles.

The Aggregate Question

While per-user streaming emissions are tiny, the aggregate is meaningful:

  • Global data centers use ~1–1.5% of global electricity
  • Video streaming is ~60% of internet traffic
  • Netflix alone serves 260+ million subscribers

But here's the key: data centers are getting far more efficient. Global internet traffic has grown 25x since 2010, but data center energy use has only grown ~10% thanks to massive efficiency gains.

How to Stream More Sustainably

  1. Don't stream 4K on small screens — You can't tell the difference on a phone. Use SD/HD instead.
  2. Download instead of re-stream — If you rewatch, download once.
  3. Turn off autoplay — Prevents background streaming you're not watching.
  4. Use Wi-Fi over cellular — Wi-Fi is ~5x more energy-efficient than 4G/5G.
  5. Don't worry about it — Seriously. Focus on transport, diet, and energy. Streaming is a rounding error.
